Learning Management System Project

User Experience & User Interface Design
Overview

This past summer I took a course on User Experience Design through iXperience, an education startup targeted at college students from the US interested in learning applied technical skills often not taught in many liberal arts colleges. My course consisted of 15 students and was taught by Megan Adolf, an industry UX professional based out of Chicago. The premise of our course was to learn the various aspects of the UX design process through an accelerated simulation project. Our task was to research and design an overhauled Learning Management System (LMS) solution for iXperience based on the company’s core values and objectives.

The Problem

The biggest frustration we found expressed by students and educators was that most LMS’s work “just fine” but aren’t great. Nobody is in love with their LMS like they might be with apss Instagram or Slack. When we dug a little deeper we found this to be the general feeling due to the fact that most Learning Management Systems are one size fits all, used by a variety of schools and in a variety of courses. There are big players like Moodle, Blackboard, and Canvas (which my summer course and university both use) that we researched and interviewed students and educators about, and found that overall they do the job just fine, however, educators usually resorted to using auxiliary products as well to fill their specific needs and styles, since the all-around LMS’s seemed to fall short for specific cases. As a result, the educational software for many classes often became a collection of various website and applications, with the inefficiency of various logins and lack of cross-platform integrations. Since our project was to create a custom LMS for iXperience, we focused on how we could incorporate the specific needs of its teachers while incorporating familiar and successful characteristics found in other software offerings.

The Process

Since our class was taught through work on the project, we conducted interviews with students, teachers, and administrators both in the program and from home then developed personas and finally user stories we used to create flows for. We then drafted paper prototypes we tested with our peers from other courses and made several iterations on these designs. Moving into the wireframing phase, I was grouped with two other classmates and in our group we explored our specific ideas for the concept for which we then refined and developed hi fidelity, clickable prototypes using InVision.

Deliverables

Over the duration of the four-week course, we produced student personas and journey maps, user flows, paper prototypes, wireframes, and finally high fidelity mockups of the web based interface which we then presented to our class as well as the CEO and Head of Product of the company. Our concept was then chosen as one of two from the UX program to be presented at the sessions closing presentations.

Thinking Ahead

If we had additional time to work on the project, I would have spent more time user testing our final UI and developing prototypes for the mobile application. It’s essential for people to access all the necessary information they receive from class on the go, and smartphone accessibility was a necessary feature that was stressed heavily by the students we interviewed. We did a little work with mobile user flows before we moved into focused groups, and I explored a feature that used cards to allow students to quickly absorb and manage notifications and new assignments by swiping gestures to organize their “deck” of to-do items.
