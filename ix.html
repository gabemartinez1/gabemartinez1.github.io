Transformations Studio

Led by Megan Panzano, Harvard Graduate School of Design
Overview

Spring 2016

Taught by GSD professor Megan Panzano, this ten person undergraduate studio focused on the introduction to thought and creation of physical form, first in an abstract and then applied context. Moving through various iterations of an initial 8-piece paper exercise, our models “transformed”, both rapidly in digital form and then formally through our various exercises: Operations, Patterns, Intensities, Volume, and Composite. We used various processes and materials for each unit including 3D modeling in Rhinoceros, laser cutting and assembling plexi glass, chip board, and poster board, and 3D printing. After the completion of each of the five units, we presented our models as well as digital renderings to a panel of design critics, ranging from professors at the Graduate School of Design to visiting professors from area schools and working industry professionals.

Final Project

Unlike the first half of the semester which consisted of various small projects, the second half was filled with a single final project: Composite. This assignment tasked us to analyze and create a revitalized vision of our local Longfellow Park in Cambridge. This project was challenging in that the context of the park is very unique, bounded by two high traffic streets and residential and community use buildings, creating a think greenspace that exists on physical levels to connect the upper area of one street to the lower area of the river shore.

I focused my reimagining of the space on the drastic level change that occurs in the middle of the park, facilitated by symmetrical staircases situated on the sides of a memorial to Longfellow himself. Through many observations, I found that though the park is laid out in a symmetrical fashion, its visitors, mainly humans and dogs, experience the park in an unlimited amount of random pathways that are restricted only by the stairs and a narrow gate opening onto Mount Auburn Street.

My final model and analytical drawings explored the possibility of a mix use community structure to be built atop the existing land as well as mesh with the earth itself through the use of organic building materials. I was inspired by the random pathways of the park visitors to create walkways that explored the concept of level as well as broke the symmetry the park was originally designed with.
